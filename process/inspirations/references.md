# Inspirations & References

## Directly inspired by

- **Creative Commons** — The two-layer structure (human-readable summary + legal text) is directly borrowed from CC's approach. CC also proved that a new license can become globally adopted if it's simple and well-designed.

- **"Beyond Open Source: Why AI-Assisted Projects Need Open Method"** (DEV Community, Jan 2026) — A blog post that articulated the exact problem OpenAll solves: when AI writes code, the code alone doesn't tell you how it was made. The author proposed "Open Method" as a community norm. OpenAll takes this further by making it a formal license. https://dev.to/olaproeis/beyond-open-source-why-ai-assisted-projects-need-open-method-31pl

- **Anthropic's Constitutional AI** — The idea that AI governance rules can be amended through deliberation influenced OpenAll's governance model (Section 8), where both humans and AI can propose license amendments.

## Broader context

- **MIT License** — Admired for its simplicity. OpenAll aims for the same "you can read it in 2 minutes" quality in the human-readable summary.

- **GPL / Copyleft tradition** — We studied the GPL's copyleft mechanism but ultimately chose a permissive approach. Copyleft creates adoption friction that would work against our goal of widespread process transparency.

- **OSI's Open Source AI Definition (OSAID)** — The broader effort to define what "open" means for AI. OpenAll addresses a different angle: not the openness of AI models, but the transparency of using AI to build software.

- **Red Hat's AI attribution guidelines** — Their suggestion to use "Assisted-by:" commit trailers influenced our thinking, though OpenAll goes much further by requiring full conversation logs.

## Anti-inspirations

- **"Open source washing"** — Companies calling things "open source" while keeping the important parts closed. OpenAll is a direct response to this: if you're not showing your process, you're not truly open.
